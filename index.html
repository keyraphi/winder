01.Winding Numbers: Introduction & Topological Foundations

Computing spatial queries—such as determining whether a localized query coordinate sits inside or outside an arbitrary geometric shell—is a foundational requirement in geometric processing, 3D computer vision, and physical simulators. Traditionally, this is straightforward for watertight, perfectly clean manifold topologies. However, raw real-world data presents severe challenges, often appearing as fractured triangle soups, open boundaries, or raw unorganized point arrays.

To resolve this ambiguity, the Generalized Winding Number framework maps spatial points to a continuous volumetric field. Rather than enforcing strict binary boundaries, it evaluates the solid angle subtended by the surface primitives relative to the evaluation site $x \in \mathbb{R}^3$. For any continuous, closed manifold surface $\mathcal{M}$, the continuous winding field evaluator calculates precisely $1$ inside the core container and $0$ outside:

$$\mathcal{W}(x) = \frac{1}{4\pi} \iint_{\mathcal{M}} \frac{(p - x) \cdot n(p)}{\|p - x\|^3} \, dA(p)$$

Where $x$ is the 3D spatial evaluation query point, $p$ is a integration coordinate lying on the surface geometry $\mathcal{M}$, $n(p)$ denotes the outward-facing unit normal vector at position $p$, and $dA(p)$ represents the infinitesimal area element.

When dealing with digital geometry, this continuous formulation must be discretized. For standard Triangle Soups (unoriented, non-manifold, or disconnected meshes), the total winding number at query site $x$ is evaluated as the sum of signed solid angles $\Omega_t$ subtended by each individual triangle $t$ within the collection $\mathcal{T}$:

$$\mathcal{W}_{\text{mesh}}(x) = \frac{1}{4\pi} \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} \Omega_t(x)$$

To guarantee numerical stability near triangle edges, vertices, or coplanar configurations, $\Omega_t(x)$ is computed robustly using the reliable signed solid angle formulation established by Van Oosterom and Strackee (1983). For a triangle with vertex positions defined relative to the query point as $a = v_1 - x$, $b = v_2 - x$, and $c = v_3 - x$, the solid angle is computed via:

$$\tan\left(\frac{\Omega_t(x)}{2}\right) = \frac{a \cdot (b \times c)}{\|a\|\|b\|\|c\| + (a \cdot b)\|c\| + (b \cdot c)\|a\| + (c \cdot a)\|b\|}$$

For raw Point Clouds lacking explicit connectivity, the surface is treated as a distribution of point dipoles. The continuous integration reduces to a discrete sum over point samples $i$:

$$\mathcal{W}_{\text{points}}(x) = \frac{1}{4\pi} \sum_{i} \frac{(p_i - x) \cdot n_i}{\|p_i - x\|^3} a_i$$

Where $p_i$ represents the 3D spatial position of point sample $i$, $n_i$ represents its oriented unit normal vector, and $a_i$ denotes the localized surface area weight associated with that sample.

By framing inside-outside segmentation through continuous integration, this formulation is highly resilient to topological defects, self-intersections, and structural holes. This mathematical foundation allows researchers to evaluate unstructured data pipelines safely, as highlighted in the seminal work on Robust Inside-Outside Segmentation using Generalized Winding Numbers (Jacobson et al., 2013).

02. Hierarchical Acceleration & Taylor Expansions

1. The Computational Bottleneck

Evaluating the exact discrete winding number formula requires computing the signed solid angle or dipole potential between every single query location and every surface primitive. This means computing the winding numbers for $M$ query points and $N$ geometric source primitives has a computational complexity of $\mathcal{O}(N\cdot M)$. With $M$ and $N$ goint into the millions for real world use cases this computational complexity turns into a serious bottleneck in any pipeline.

To mitigate this, earlier spatial indexing approaches attempted to use exact hierarchical structures. Notably, the divide-and-conquer framework introduced by Jacobson et al. (2013) leverages a bounding volume hierarchy (BVH) to group together manifold sub parts of the mesh. However, this method's algorithmic performance is highly dependent on clean, continuous mesh connectivity. When confronted with unoriented triangle soups, disconnected components, or broken geometry boundaries, their traversal can break down under worst-case inputs, degrading directly back to a brute-force $\mathcal{O}(nm)$ summation loop. This limitation makes large-scale processing of raw, non-manifold assets highly inefficient.

2. The Fast Winding Numbers Approximation

To resolve this scaling wall, Barill et al. (2018) introduced the Fast Winding Numbers framework. This methodology shifts the problem from an exact evaluation to a carefully constructed, hierarchical approximation. It is a tree-based algorithm in the Barnes-Hut family and builds a spatial hierarchy (such as an octree) to group distant geometric zones into structured bounding cells.

Instead of computing contribution from individual geometry elements in the far-field, the collective contribution of a distant cluster is evaluated from its shared center of mass $\tilde{p}$. While the related Fast Multipole Method (FMM) can achieve $\mathcal{O}(M)$ scaling, Barill et al. demonstrated that FMM incurs highly prohibitive precomputation overhead and relies on strict structural distributions that raw surface point distributions rarely satisfy.

The core breakthrough of the Fast Winding Numbers approach lies in treating the evaluation of a cluster of area-weighted dipoles as a truncated Taylor series expansion of the Laplace Green's function derivatives, evaluated with respect to the source position about the node's center of mass $\tilde{p}$.

Point Cloud Expansion Formulation

For a node containing a cluster of $K$ area-weighted point dipoles, Barill et al. approximate the collective winding number $\tilde{w}$ at an evaluation query coordinate $q$ via the following expansion:

$$\begin{align*}\tilde{w}(q) \approx & \left( \sum_{i=1}^{K} a_i \hat{n}_i \right) \cdot \nabla G(q, \tilde{p}) + \\ & \left( \sum_{i=1}^{K} a_i (p_i - \tilde{p}) \otimes \hat{n}_i \right) \cdot \nabla^2 G(q, \tilde{p}) +\\ & \frac{1}{2} \left( \sum_{i=1}^{K} a_i (p_i - \tilde{p}) \otimes (p_i - \tilde{p}) \otimes \hat{n}_i \right) \cdot \nabla^3 G(q, \tilde{p})\end{align*}$$

Where the operations $\otimes$ and $\cdot$ denote the tensor outer product and inner tensor contraction respectively. Here, $q$ is the 3D evaluation query point, $\tilde{p}$ is the precomputed center of mass of the target tree node, $p_i$ is the position of point sample $i$, $\hat{n}_i$ is its oriented unit normal, and $a_i$ is its localized area weight. The terms $\nabla G$, $\nabla^2 G$, and $\nabla^3 G$ represent the gradient (a vector), the Hessian (a symmetric two-tensor), and the third-derivative (a three-tensor) of the standard fundamental Green's function for the Laplace equation, evaluated between the query point and the center of mass.

The structural coefficients grouped inside the parentheses depend entirely on internal node positions relative to $\tilde{p}$. They are independent of the query position $q$, meaning they can be precomputed once during tree construction and cached inside each non-empty node.

Triangle Soup Expansion Formulation

This Taylor series framework scales naturally to raw Triangle Soups by substitute the discrete point sums inside the expansion coefficients with continuous surface integrals evaluated across the flat area elements of each triangle $t$ inside the node:

$$\begin{align*}\tilde{w}(q) \approx & \left( \sum_{t=1}^{K} \int_{t} \hat{n}_t \, dA \right) \cdot \nabla G(q, \tilde{p}) +\\ & \left( \sum_{t=1}^{K} \int_{t} (x - \tilde{p}) \otimes \hat{n}_t \, dA \right) \cdot \nabla^2 G(q, \tilde{p}) +\\ & \frac{1}{2} \left( \sum_{t=1}^{K} \int_{t} (x - \tilde{p}) \otimes (x - \tilde{p}) \otimes \hat{n}_t \, dA \right) \cdot \nabla^3 G(q, \tilde{p})\end{align*}$$

Where $\hat{n}_t$ represents the surface normal of triangle $t$, $x$ represents the continuous integration coordinate tracking across the triangle's surface area element $dA$, and $\tilde{p}$ remains the fixed center of mass of the node. The first coefficient maps out the total area-weighted normal across the grouped geometry, projecting the intuition that a distant cluster of triangles can be approximated as a single, representative larger primitive. Crucially, all three of these continuous spatial integrals possess exact, numerically stable closed-form expressions for triangles.

Asymptotic Complexity Mapping

The expansion can theoretically be extended to higher orders indefinitely. However Barill eg al. found that, in practice, expanding beyond the second order ($p=2$) yields diminishing accuracy returns relative to the computational overhead.

Evaluating a $p$-th order Taylor expansion adds a localized tensor processing complexity scaling at $\mathcal{O}(3^p)$ operations per evaluated node. Assuming a well-formed tree structure, the total amortized complexity encompassing both the structural precomputations and far-field evaluations across $n$ queries on a dataset of $m$ geometric primitives drops to:

$$\mathcal{O}(3^p N \log N + 3^p M \log N)$$

This log-linear complexity allows the Fast Winding Numbers formulation to maintain high processing speeds when evaluating massive meshes, unoriented point clouds, and raw geometric soups containing millions of elements.

04. Winder: Core Vision, Architectural Philosophy & Hardware Co-Design

1. The Construction-First Paradigm

Existing compute pipelines treating fast winding numbers rely on offline-computed spatial structures. For instance, the recent Dipole Sums implementation establishes an incredibly tight, deeply optimized Octree layout. While this minimizes traversal steps, it incurs a crippling generation bottleneck (often taking hundreds of seconds) that completely breaks viability for deep learning optimization loops or real-time dynamic geometry. Note: This extreme build time is largely an artifact of an unparallelized, single-threaded reference implementation rather than an inherent topological limitation of Octrees. Indeed, the original Fast Winding Numbers framework demonstrates a significantly more performant tree-building routine. However, it underscores a systemic architectural risk: prioritizing downstream traversal density at the cost of sequential upstream construction bottlenecks.

Winder was designed with an inverted priority system: spatial hierarchy generation must be virtually free. Rather than chasing a perfect spatial partition offline, the architecture targets millisecond, GPU-native tree builds. Everything else in the library - the precision handling, layout design, and mathematical formulation—is a direct architectural consequence built to exploit this lightning-fast, construction-first engine baseline.

2. Warp-Cooperative Leaf Design & Spatial Penalties

To achieve this millisecond build speed, Winder utilizes a parallel Linear Bounding Volume Hierarchy (LBVH) converted into a wide 8-ary structure (BVH8). To ensure tree generation scales flawlessly inside parallel GPU sorting kernels, the leaf capacity is strictly locked to exactly 32 primitives.

This leaf size choice is a deliberate hardware play. Because an NVIDIA GPU warp consists of exactly 32 execution lanes, a 32-element leaf structure unlocks massive "warp magic":

  • Zero Intra-Warp Divergence: Whole warps cooperatively traverse down the tree and unroll leaf evaluations simultaneously, completely bypassing execution lane stalls.
  • Hardware Registries & Shuffles: Internal evaluation components utilize lightning-fast register-level data shuffling (`__shfl_sync`), mitigating costly localized VRAM and shared memory round trips.

The Structural Penalty: Locking leaf sizes to 32 items to maximize build speed forces a trade-off in spatial efficiency. In regions with highly sparse geometry, these leaves can become spatially oversized and loose, failing to bound the underlying points as tightly as an offline-built Octree would.

3. Compounding Speed: Compensating via Hardware Quantization

Because the spatial bounding structure is intentionally loose to favor ultra-fast generation, the evaluation pass faces a higher mathematical workload. Winder systematically counteracts this spatial penalty not by making the boxes tighter, but by making the mathematics handling them blisteringly cheap through extreme hardware co-design:

  • Second-Order Taylor Expansions: Because the 32-primitive leaf footprint delays the onset of far-field clustering, the engine naturally evaluates more interactions exactly rather than approximating them. Where hierarchical approximations are triggered, Winder uses a second-order Taylor series. While the library's downstream quantization keeps its absolute precision below the pure float32 second-order baseline of original Fast Winding Numbers, it provides a tighter mathematical fidelity profile than zero-order dipole frameworks.
  • bfloat16 Far-Field Math: All calculations in the far-field are done in the bfloat16 floating-point format, leading to high ALU computing throughput.
  • Bit-Packed Mantissas & 128-Byte Cache Alignment: Tree node coefficients are aggressively bit-packed down to a custom 11-bit mantissa format with shared exponents, while spatial metadata—such as the cluster center of mass and localized bounding radii—are compressed to an 8-bit format. This strictly constrains the entire BVH8 node payload to exactly 128 bytes, perfectly matching the native L2 cache line transaction size of modern NVIDIA GPUs to guarantee maximum memory bandwidth utilization.

4. Dynamic Query-Tree Inversion & Mathematical Backpropagation

To compute the derivative fields of generalized winding numbers for reverse-mode automatic differentiation, we map an incoming scalar adjoint gradient $g_j = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w(x_j)} \in \mathbb{R}$ at each query position $x_j$ back to the underlying spatial geometry. Depending on the input representation, the analytical vector gradients ($\mathbb{R}^3$) for the three primitive configurations are defined as:

1. Gradient w.r.t. Scaled Normals (Dipole Moments $m_i = a_i \cdot n_i \in \mathbb{R}^3$): $$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial m_i} = \sum_{j=1}^{M} g_j \nabla G(x_j - p_i)$$
2. Gradient w.r.t. Source Point Positions ($p_i \in \mathbb{R}^3$): $$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial p_i} = -\sum_{j=1}^{M} g_j \mathbf{H}_G(x_j - p_i) m_i$$
3. Gradient w.r.t. Triangle Vertices ($v_k \in \mathbb{R}^3$): $$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial v_k} = \sum_{j=1}^{M} g_j \frac{\partial \Omega_T(x_j)}{\partial v_k}$$

Evaluating these formulations directly requires a dense pairwise computation between all $N$ geometry elements and all $M$ query points, yielding an unoptimized computational complexity of $\mathcal{O}(NM)$.

However, the underlying field behavior of the backward pass closely mirrors the forward pass. While the forward pass clusters geometry sources because their collective potential field varies smoothly over distance, the backward pass allows us to cluster query points. The adjoint fields, specifically the free-space Green's function gradient $\nabla G$, its spatial Hessian $\mathbf{H}_G$, and the boundary derivative vector field, are smooth fields that decay rapidly over space. Consequently, the influence of a distant cluster of query points on any given source primitive is highly predictable, making it well-suited for a reverse hierarchical approximation scheme.

Truncated Taylor Expansions for Primitive Derivatives

Case 1: Scaled Normals (Dipole Moments $m_i$)

Expanding the vector dipole gradient field $\nabla G(x_j - p_i)$ for a single source point $p_i$ relative to the distant query cluster centroid $\tilde{q}$ yields the multi-order tensor series:

$$\begin{align*}\nabla G(x_j - p_i) \approx & \nabla G(\tilde{q} - p_i) +\\ & \mathbf{H}_G(\tilde{q} - p_i)(x_j - \tilde{q}) + \\ & \frac{1}{2} \nabla^3 G(\tilde{q} - p_i) : \left[ (x_j - \tilde{q}) \otimes (x_j - \tilde{q}) \right]\end{align*}$$

Distributing the summation over the query indices $j$ isolates the geometry-independent parameters inside the parentheses:

$$\begin{align*}\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial m_i} \approx & \nabla G(\tilde{q} - p_i) \left( \sum_j g_j \right) + \\ & \mathbf{H}_G(\tilde{q} - p_i) \left( \sum_j g_j (x_j - \tilde{q}) \right) +\\ & \frac{1}{2} \nabla^3 G(\tilde{q} - p_i) : \left( \sum_j g_j (x_j - \tilde{q}) \otimes (x_j - \tilde{q}) \right)\end{align*}$$

Note: The target gradient field output is a vector in $\mathbb{R}^3$. Inside the expansion, the first term scales a vector ($\mathbb{R}^3$) by a scalar; the second multiplies a matrix ($\mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}$) by a vector; the third double-contracts ($:$) a 3rd-order tensor ($\mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3 \times 3}$) against a second-order outer-product matrix ($\mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}$).

Case 2: Point Positions ($p_i$)

Because the position gradient natively evaluates through the matrix-valued potential Hessian $\mathbf{H}_G(x_j - p_i)$, we construct the Taylor series of this second-order tensor field around the cluster centroid $\tilde{q}$:

$$\begin{align*}\mathbf{H}_G(x_j - p_i) \approx & \mathbf{H}_G(\tilde{q} - p_i) +\\ & \nabla^3 G(\tilde{q} - p_i) \cdot (x_j - \tilde{q}) + \\ & \frac{1}{2} \nabla^4 G(\tilde{q} - p_i) : \left[ (x_j - \tilde{q}) \otimes (x_j - \tilde{q}) \right]\end{align*}$$

Factoring out the invariant source parameters and the dipole vector $m_i$, distributing the scalar loss adjoint $g_j$ isolates our unified query moments inside the contractions:

$$\begin{align*}\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial p_i} \approx - \Biggl[ & \mathbf{H}_G(\tilde{q} - p_i) \left( \sum_j g_j \right) + \\ & \nabla^3 G(\tilde{q} - p_i) \cdot \left( \sum_j g_j (x_j - \tilde{q}) \right) + \\ & \frac{1}{2} \nabla^4 G(\tilde{q} - p_i) : \left( \sum_j g_j (x_j - \tilde{q}) \otimes (x_j - \tilde{q}) \right) \Biggr] m_i\end{align*}$$

Note: The bracketed terms resolve to a second-order matrix ($\mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}$). The first term scales the baseline matrix by a scalar ($G_0$); the second contracts a 3rd-order tensor against a vector ($G_1$) via a single dot product; the third reduces a 4th-order tensor against a matrix ($G_2$) via a double contraction. Post-multiplying the resulting matrix by the vector $m_i \in \mathbb{R}^3$ yields the final derivative vector in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

Case 3: Triangle Vertices ($v_k$)

Denoting the local vertex boundary derivative vector field as $F_k(x_j) = \frac{\partial \Omega_T(x_j)}{\partial v_k} \in \mathbb{R}^3$, we express its Taylor serialization about $\tilde{q}$ directly as:

$$\begin{align*}F_k(x_j) \approx & F_k(\tilde{q}) +\\ & \nabla F_k(\tilde{q})(x_j - \tilde{q}) + \\ & \frac{1}{2} \mathbf{H}_{F_k}(\tilde{q}) : \left[ (x_j - \tilde{q}) \otimes (x_j - \tilde{q}) \right]\end{align*}$$

Distributing the scalar loss adjoint $g_j$ yields the localized structural summation form:

$$\begin{align*}\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial v_k} \approx & F_k(\tilde{q}) \left( \sum_j g_j \right) + \\ & \nabla F_k(\tilde{q}) \left( \sum_j g_j (x_j - \tilde{q}) \right) + \\ & \frac{1}{2} \mathbf{H}_{F_k}(\tilde{q}) : \left( \sum_j g_j (x_j - \tilde{q}) \otimes (x_j - \tilde{q}) \right)\end{align*}$$

Note: Because the baseline field $F_k$ maps directly to a vector, its spatial gradient $\nabla F_k$ is a matrix $(\mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3})$ and its Hessian $\mathbf{H}_{F_k}$ is a 3rd-order tensor $(\mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3 \times 3})$. The expression smoothly resolves to $\mathbb{R}^3$ via vector-scalar scaling, matrix-vector multiplication, and a double tensor contraction ($:$) against the matrix-valued displacement outer product.

The Inversion Convergence: Unified Query Moments

The critical revelation of this parallel derivation is revealed by inspecting the encapsulated terms in parentheses across all three primitive spaces. These are the components that are entirely independent of the source geometry parameters and can be completely precomputed. Remarkably, the summation configurations match perfectly in every single equation.

All derivatives can be simultaneously computed using the exact same three multi-order Query Moments cached within the hierarchy layout:

0th-Order Moment (Scalar $G_0 \in \mathbb{R}$) $$G_0 = \sum_{j} g_j$$
1st-Order Moment (Vector $G_1 \in \mathbb{R}^3$) $$G_1 = \sum_{j} g_j (x_j - \tilde{q})$$
2nd-Order Moment (Matrix $G_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}$) $$G_2 = \sum_{j} g_j (x_j - \tilde{q}) \otimes (x_j - \tilde{q})$$

By constructing the tree over the query coordinates on the fly, a single bottom-up parallel prefix pass calculates these moments. During execution, geometry elements act as tree traversers, evaluating field derivatives through local $\mathcal{O}(1)$ contractions against the cached $G_0$, $G_1$, and $G_2$ node blocks. This limits the backward computational pass strictly to a highly efficient execution complexity of $\mathcal{O}((M + N) \log M)$, which natively accounts for the initial $\mathcal{O}(M)$ tree construction overhead.
