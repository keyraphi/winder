01. Introduction & Topological Foundations

Computing spatial queries—such as determining whether a localized query coordinate sits inside or outside an arbitrary geometric shell—is a foundational requirement in geometric processing, 3D computer vision, and physical simulators. Traditionally, this is straightforward for watertight, perfectly clean manifold topologies. However, raw real-world data presents severe challenges, often appearing as fractured triangle soups, open boundaries, or raw unorganized point arrays.

To resolve this ambiguity, the Generalized Winding Number framework maps spatial points to a continuous volumetric field. Rather than enforcing strict binary boundaries, it evaluates the solid angle subtended by the surface primitives relative to the evaluation site $x \in \mathbb{R}^3$. For any continuous, closed manifold surface $\mathcal{M}$, the continuous winding field evaluator calculates precisely $1$ inside the core container and $0$ outside:

$$\mathcal{W}(x) = \frac{1}{4\pi} \iint_{\mathcal{M}} \frac{(p - x) \cdot n(p)}{\|p - x\|^3} \, dA(p)$$

Where $x$ is the 3D spatial evaluation query point, $p$ is a integration coordinate lying on the surface geometry $\mathcal{M}$, $n(p)$ denotes the outward-facing unit normal vector at position $p$, and $dA(p)$ represents the infinitesimal area element.

When dealing with digital geometry, this continuous formulation must be discretized. For standard Triangle Soups (unoriented, non-manifold, or disconnected meshes), the total winding number at query site $x$ is evaluated as the sum of signed solid angles $\Omega_t$ subtended by each individual triangle $t$ within the collection $\mathcal{T}$:

$$\mathcal{W}_{\text{mesh}}(x) = \frac{1}{4\pi} \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} \Omega_t(x)$$

To guarantee numerical stability near triangle edges, vertices, or coplanar configurations, $\Omega_t(x)$ is computed robustly using the reliable signed solid angle formulation established by Van Oosterom and Strackee (1983). For a triangle with vertex positions defined relative to the query point as $a = v_1 - x$, $b = v_2 - x$, and $c = v_3 - x$, the solid angle is computed via:

$$\tan\left(\frac{\Omega_t(x)}{2}\right) = \frac{a \cdot (b \times c)}{\|a\|\|b\|\|c\| + (a \cdot b)\|c\| + (b \cdot c)\|a\| + (c \cdot a)\|b\|}$$

For raw Point Clouds lacking explicit connectivity, the surface is treated as a distribution of point dipoles. The continuous integration reduces to a discrete sum over point samples $i$:

$$\mathcal{W}_{\text{points}}(x) = \frac{1}{4\pi} \sum_{i} \frac{(p_i - x) \cdot n_i}{\|p_i - x\|^3} a_i$$

Where $p_i$ represents the 3D spatial position of point sample $i$, $n_i$ represents its oriented unit normal vector, and $a_i$ denotes the localized surface area weight associated with that sample.

By framing inside-outside segmentation through continuous integration, this formulation is highly resilient to topological defects, self-intersections, and structural holes. This mathematical foundation allows researchers to evaluate unstructured data pipelines safely, as highlighted in the seminal work on Robust Inside-Outside Segmentation using Generalized Winding Numbers (Jacobson et al., 2013).

02. Hierarchical Acceleration & Taylor Expansions

1. The Computational Bottleneck

Evaluating the exact discrete winding number formula requires computing the signed solid angle or dipole potential between every single query location and every surface primitive. For a tracking scenario featuring $n$ query points and $m$ geometric source primitives, this exhaustive direct evaluation introduces a rigid computational bottleneck scaling at $\mathcal{O}(nm)$ complexity.

To mitigate this, earlier spatial indexing approaches attempted to use exact hierarchical structures. Notably, the divide-and-conquer framework introduced by Jacobson et al. (2013) leverages a bounding volume hierarchy (BVH) to compute winding values exactly. However, this method's algorithmic performance is highly dependent on clean, continuous mesh connectivity. When confronted with unoriented triangle soups, disconnected components, or broken geometry boundaries, this exact traversal can break down under worst-case inputs, degrading directly back to a brute-force $\mathcal{O}(nm)$ summation loop. This limitation makes large-scale processing of raw, non-manifold industrial assets highly inefficient.

2. The Fast Winding Numbers Approximation

To resolve this scaling wall, Barill et al. (2018) introduced the Fast Winding Numbers framework. This methodology shifts the problem from an exact evaluation to a highly controlled, hierarchical approximation. Classified as a tree-based algorithm in the Barnes-Hut family, it builds a spatial hierarchy (such as an octree) to group distant geometric zones into structured bounding cells.

Instead of computing potentials from individual elements inside far-field nodes, the collective contribution of a distant cluster is evaluated from its shared center of mass $\tilde{p}$. While the related Fast Multipole Method (FMM) can achieve $\mathcal{O}(n)$ scaling, Barill et al. demonstrated that FMM incurs highly prohibitive precomputation overhead and relies on strict structural distributions that raw surface point distributions rarely satisfy.

The core breakthrough of the Fast Winding Numbers approach lies in treating the evaluation of a cluster of area-weighted dipoles as a truncated Taylor series expansion of the Laplace Green's function derivatives, evaluated with respect to the source position about the node's center of mass $\tilde{p}$.

Point Cloud Expansion Formulation

For a node containing a cluster of $m$ area-weighted point dipoles, Barill et al. approximate the collective winding number $\tilde{w}$ at an evaluation query coordinate $q$ via the following expansion:

$$\begin{align*}\tilde{w}(q) \approx & \left( \sum_{i=1}^{m} a_i \hat{n}_i \right) \cdot \nabla G(q, \tilde{p}) + \\ & \left( \sum_{i=1}^{m} a_i (p_i - \tilde{p}) \otimes \hat{n}_i \right) \cdot \nabla^2 G(q, \tilde{p}) +\\ & \frac{1}{2} \left( \sum_{i=1}^{m} a_i (p_i - \tilde{p}) \otimes (p_i - \tilde{p}) \otimes \hat{n}_i \right) \cdot \nabla^3 G(q, \tilde{p})\end{align*}$$

Where the operations $\otimes$ and $\cdot$ denote the tensor outer product and inner tensor contraction respectively. Here, $q$ is the 3D evaluation query point, $\tilde{p}$ is the precomputed center of mass of the target tree node, $p_i$ is the position of point sample $i$, $\hat{n}_i$ is its oriented unit normal, and $a_i$ is its localized area weight. The terms $\nabla G$, $\nabla^2 G$, and $\nabla^3 G$ represent the gradient (a vector), the Hessian (a symmetric two-tensor), and the third-derivative (a three-tensor) of the standard fundamental Green's function for the Laplace equation, evaluated between the query point and the center of mass.

The structural coefficients grouped inside the parentheses depend entirely on internal node positions relative to $\tilde{p}$. They are independent of the query position $q$, meaning they can be precomputed once during tree construction and cached inside each non-empty node.

Triangle Soup Expansion Formulation

This Taylor series framework scales naturally to raw Triangle Soups. Because differentiation and integration are linear operators that associate, Barill et al. substitute the discrete point sums inside the expansion coefficients with continuous surface integrals evaluated across the flat area elements of each triangle $t$ inside the node:

$$\begin{align*}\tilde{w}(q) \approx & \left( \sum_{t=1}^{m} \int_{t} \hat{n}_t \, dA \right) \cdot \nabla G(q, \tilde{p}) +\\ & \left( \sum_{t=1}^{m} \int_{t} (x - \tilde{p}) \otimes \hat{n}_t \, dA \right) \cdot \nabla^2 G(q, \tilde{p}) +\\ & \frac{1}{2} \left( \sum_{t=1}^{m} \int_{t} (x - \tilde{p}) \otimes (x - \tilde{p}) \otimes \hat{n}_t \, dA \right) \cdot \nabla^3 G(q, \tilde{p})\end{align*}$$

Where $\hat{n}_t$ represents the surface normal of triangle $t$, $x$ represents the continuous integration coordinate tracking across the triangle's surface area element $dB$, and $\tilde{p}$ remains the fixed center of mass of the cluster. The first coefficient maps out the total area-weighted normal across the grouped geometry, projecting the intuition that a distant cluster of triangles can be approximated as a single, representative larger primitive. Crucially, all three of these continuous spatial integrals possess exact, numerically stable closed-form expressions.

Asymptotic Complexity Mapping

The expansion can theoretically be extended to higher orders indefinitely. However Barill eg al. found that, in practice, expanding beyond the second order ($p=2$) yields diminishing accuracy returns relative to the computational overhead.

Evaluating a $p$-th order Taylor expansion adds a localized tensor processing complexity scaling at $\mathcal{O}(3^p)$ operations per evaluated node. Assuming a well-formed tree structure, the total amortized complexity encompassing both the structural precomputations and far-field evaluations across $n$ queries on a dataset of $m$ geometric primitives drops to:

$$\mathcal{O}(3^p m \log m + 3^p n \log m)$$

This log-linear complexity allows the Fast Winding Numbers formulation to maintain high processing speeds when evaluating massive CAD assemblies, unoriented point clouds, and raw geometric soups containing millions of elements.

04. Winder: Core Vision, Architectural Philosophy & Hardware Co-Design

1. Mathematical Motivation

Generalized winding numbers represent an exceptionally elegant paradigm in geometric computing. Beyond their topological resilience, winding fields are inherently smooth, harmonic, fully differentiable, and completely decoupled across spatial queries. Because every evaluation point is independent of its neighbors, the formulation presents an ideal profile for massive parallelization on modern GPU architectures.

Despite these advantages, existing implementations often fail to balance mathematical elegance with modern computing realities. Winder was conceived to bridge this gap—functioning as a definitive, unified, and highly optimized GPU-native library that resolves winding number evaluations and their derivatives within a single high-performance framework.

2. Hardware-Driven Approximation & Aggressive Quantization

During architectural design, the development of Winder intentionally pivoted from chasing infinite mathematical precision toward optimizing for memory-bandwidth saturation and compute throughput on the GPU. To maximize execution speed, the library introduces several strategic numerical trade-offs:

  • Reduced-Precision Arithmetic: Far-field approximation mathematics are executed completely within the bfloat16 floating-point format, vastly accelerating ALU throughput at the cost of low-frequency truncation errors.
  • Coefficient Bit-Packing: The higher-order Taylor expansion coefficients stored within each internal tree node are quantized down to an 11-bit mantissa configuration, maximizing cache density and preventing memory fetches from stalling the execution pipelines.
  • Strict Memory Alignment: Spatial metadata—specifically the cluster center of mass $\tilde{p}$ and the localized bounding radius tracking contained primitives—are compressed and quantized into strict 8-byte boundaries to maintain clean, coalesced memory access patterns across GPU warp paths.

These deliberate quantization strategies yield a coarser numerical approximation profile than the original CPU-bound *Fast Winding Numbers* algorithm by Barill et al. However, by preserving a second-order Taylor expansion structure, Winder retains tighter geometric accuracy than zero-order dipole implementations, while achieving similar evaluation speeds.

3. Symmetric Dual Traversal for Gradient Computation

Evaluating the derivative fields of winding numbers for backward differentiation presents a structural inversion challenge: computing gradients with respect to the source geometry is mathematically the inverse operation of evaluating forward potentials at query positions.

To resolve this efficiently, Winder implements a symmetric dual-traversal architecture. Instead of evaluating source clusters against query locations, the engine constructs a highly parallelized GPU Radix Tree directly over the query coordinates. The source points or triangle primitives then traverse this query tree hierarchically, accumulating analytical derivatives back into the geometry indices in an upside-down fashion. This traversal-friendly data structure enables ultra-fast tree building via parallelized Morton code sorting, delivering an incredibly robust and highly scalable pipeline for geometric optimization.

4. Sub-Millisecond Hierarchy Generation & Wide BVH Layouts

A core priority in the development of Winder was eliminating the spatial data structure generation bottleneck, which frequently cripples the performance of deep learning pipelines requiring dynamic runtime geometry updates. Inspired by the hardware-tailored approaches of Jakob, Johannes & Guthe, Michael (Optimizing LBVH‐Construction and Hierarchy‐Traversal to accelerate kNN Queries on Point Clouds using the GPU), the spatial backend leverages a highly optimized Linear Bounding Volume Hierarchy (LBVH) baseline.

This initial linear hierarchy is subsequently refactored and compressed into a wide 8-ary bounding volume structure (BVH8). Reconfiguring the tree to support an 8-child node branching factor drastically flattens the total traversal depth, maximizes SIMD/warp execution lane utilization, and eliminates redundant execution divergence during the deep-level tree lookups executed by the GPU.

Engine Pipeline & Features

High-performance hardware design choices engineered for massive geometric datasets.

⚡ Morton-Encoded LBVH

Constructs linear binary bounding hierarchies via 64-bit Morton codes using an ultra-fast radix tree structure mapped entirely on GPU blocks, bypassing traditional CPU-side tree generation bottlenecks.

🌿 Wide BVH8 Generation

Collapses sparse binary trees into high-occupancy 8-way multi-bounding box layouts. This flat structure increases streaming vector performance and compresses depth traversal passes during execution.

🌲 Bottom-Up M2M Layers

Executes parallelized upward CUDA passes across tree layers to propagate and cascade high-order Taylor series coefficients from individual leaf elements up to the root node.

📦 128-Byte Quantized Nodes

Compresses BVH8 layout entries into compact 128-byte cache footprints using custom PTX assembly byte-quantization to maximize memory bandwidth.

🔢 Dual-Precision Tracks

Alternates dynamically between high-speed bfloat16 math operations for distant far-field approximations and robust float32 compute tracks for high-precision near-field intersections.

🤝 Cooperative Kernels

Distributes work evenly across active threads using synchronized shared-memory tracking queues, minimizing warp divergence during hierarchical tree steps.
